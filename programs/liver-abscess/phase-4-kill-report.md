# Phase 4: Kill Report -- Hepatic Abscessation in Feedlot Cattle

**Program:** Liver Abscess | **Partner:** Elanco | **Date:** 2026-03-27
**Agent:** Reaper | **Status:** Complete

---

## Executive Summary

Twenty-seven candidates entered. Two were pre-killed by Forge (#21 PDT, #15 antiplatelet). Of the remaining 25, I killed 6, wounded 12, and 7 survived.

The portfolio's fatal weakness is not any single candidate -- it is the **dose-overwhelm problem that has killed every intervention ever tried on grain diets**. Fourteen of the 27 candidates face the same fundamental challenge that destroyed Fusogard: can your intervention keep up with 150+ days of continuous bacterial translocation from a chronically damaged rumen wall? Forge acknowledges this but then proposes candidates that walk directly into it.

Three Surveyor corrections materially change the verdict on high-priority candidates:
1. **Hemagglutinin subspecies confusion** undermines Candidate 4's third antigen and every candidate referencing hemagglutinin
2. **Multi-antigen vaccine misattribution** (hemolysin H2, not hemagglutinin) means Forge cited a study that does not support its own vaccine formulation
3. **FomA-Factor H dual function is unproven** -- strips Candidate 5 of its key differentiator

**The hardest truth:** The only candidate with real-world cattle data approaching tylosin efficacy (MON+BX, #1) is still 10 percentage points worse than MON+TYL. Everything else is mice, in vitro, or inference. The portfolio is building a cathedral on a foundation of mouse data and bovine endothelial cell binding assays.

### Candidate 1: Monensin + Tannin Blend (MON+BX)

**Forge rank:** Tier 1 (highest priority)

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| 20-Year Test | PASS -- this combination is genuinely recent (Felizari 2025) |
| Species Test | PASS -- cattle data, n=2,986, commercial feedlot |
| Matrix Test | PASS -- tested in actual feedlot conditions |
| Strain Test | UNKNOWN -- tannin MIC tested against F. necrophorum but strain diversity not assessed |
| Resistance Test | LOW RISK -- polyphenolic mechanism unlikely to generate classical resistance |
| Citation Test | PASS -- Felizari et al. 2025, Vet Sci 12:446, confirmed by Surveyor and web search (PMID 40431539) |
| Stoichiometric Test | N/A |
| Embarrassment Test | **The gap persists.** MON+BX achieves 28.5% LA prevalence. MON+TYL achieves 18.3%. That is a 10.2 percentage point gap. After dose optimization, if the gap closes to 5 points, it is still 5 points. At 10,000 head, 5% means 500 additional condemned livers. Can Elanco sell a product that is measurably inferior to the product it replaces? |
| Repetition Test | PARTIAL -- BX alone is no better than control (36.8% vs 43.1%). The tannin blend alone repeats the failure of every standalone non-antibiotic approach. Only the combination with monensin approaches efficacy. |
| Commercial Reality Test | PASS -- feed additive, compatible with feedlot operations |
| Independent Replication Test | **FAIL -- single study.** One trial (Felizari 2025). n=2,986 is respectable, but this is ONE feedlot, ONE trial, ONE BX formulation. No independent replication. |
| Clinical Endpoint Test | PASS -- liver abscess prevalence at slaughter is the primary endpoint |

**The real question Forge does not answer:** The 10-point gap between MON+BX and MON+TYL -- is this a dose problem (solvable by increasing BX) or a mechanistic ceiling (tannins simply cannot do what tylosin does at the rumen wall)? If BX's mechanism is primarily slowing starch fermentation and protein binding, not direct anti-Fn activity at the wall, then no dose increase will close the gap. The MIC of 6.25-12.5 ug/mL for grape seed phenolics against F. necrophorum in broth tells you nothing about achievable concentrations at the rumen wall after protein binding has consumed most of the tannin.

**Verdict: SURVIVED**

This is the strongest candidate in the portfolio. Single-study risk is real but the study is large and well-designed. The 10-point gap is a serious commercial concern but is addressable via dose optimization and combination with other candidates. I tried to kill it on the gap and the single-study dependency. It held.

### Candidate 2: Rumen-Protected Encapsulated Sodium Butyrate

**Forge rank:** Tier 2

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| 20-Year Test | WOUNDED -- butyrate's tight junction effects have been known for >10 years. No one has tested it for liver abscess. Why not? Because the dairy industry uses it for SARA, and the feedlot industry does not invest in barrier biology research. Not a commercial indictment but a gap. |
| Species Test | PARTIAL PASS -- butyrate tight junction upregulation shown in bovine rumen epithelium (Steele et al. 2009-2012). But the dose-response in feedlot cattle on 85-90% concentrate is unknown. |
| Matrix Test | **FAIL -- never tested in feedlot conditions.** Dairy SARA studies use different diets, lower challenge levels, and different endpoints. The rumen of a finishing steer on 90% concentrate is a different environment than a dairy cow on TMR. |
| Embarrassment Test | **Butyrate is a VFA. The rumen of a grain-fed steer already contains 50-80 mM butyrate. Adding more butyrate to an organ drowning in VFAs and expecting tight junction REPAIR rather than further damage is paradoxical.** Forge acknowledges "excess butyrate may promote hyperkeratosis/parakeratosis." The dose window between therapeutic and damaging may be narrow or nonexistent in feedlot cattle. |
| Commercial Reality Test | PASS -- feed additive, existing rumen-protected products |
| Clinical Endpoint Test | FAIL -- no liver abscess endpoint data exists |

**The kill argument:** You are adding a VFA to a rumen that is already damaged by VFA excess. The AMPK-mediated tight junction upregulation is real biology, but the in vivo reality is that total VFA concentrations >150 mM are the CAUSE of barrier damage. Adding butyrate to a system where total VFAs are already supraphysiological may tip the balance toward more damage, not repair. The proposed Ussing chamber experiment is the right de-risk, but Forge's ranking of this as a top candidate is ahead of the evidence.

**Verdict: WOUNDED**

The paradox of adding a VFA to a VFA-damaged organ is a serious concern. Not killed because the Ussing chamber experiment could resolve it cheaply ($15-20K), and butyrate's signaling function is mechanistically distinct from its role as a fermentation acid. But the absence of ANY feedlot cattle data makes this speculative.

### Candidate 4: Multi-Antigen Subunit Vaccine (rFomA + rLeukotoxin PL4 + rHemagglutinin)

**Forge rank:** Tier 1 (highest priority)

This is the candidate that matters most. If it is wrong, the portfolio loses its centerpiece vaccine strategy.

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| 20-Year Test | **FAIL for leukotoxin vaccines.** Leukotoxin-based vaccination has been known since the 1990s (Fox et al. 1994, Saginala et al. 1997). Fusogard was commercialized and failed on grain diets. KSU never progressed past challenge models. This target has been known for 30 years with no grain-diet success. The specific multi-antigen combination is newer (Amachawadi 2021), but the core approach is a re-run of Fusogard with better antigens. |
| Species Test | **FAIL.** The multi-antigen vaccine (Candidate 4's reference study) was tested in BALB/c mice. Mice are insensitive to F. necrophorum leukotoxin. Mouse protection reflects mouse immune mechanisms, NOT bovine leukotoxin susceptibility. Forge acknowledges this. Surveyor confirms it. This is not a gap -- it is a fundamental species mismatch. The mouse model is MISLEADING because the primary virulence mechanism in cattle (leukotoxin-mediated neutrophil killing) does not operate in mice. Any "protection" in mice occurred via immune pathways that may be irrelevant in cattle. |
| Citation Test | **FAIL (Surveyor Correction #2).** Forge describes this vaccine as "rFomA + rLeukotoxin(PL4) + rHemagglutinin." The actual Amachawadi 2021 study used 43K OMP + PL4 + H2 (hemolysin), NOT hemagglutinin. Forge substituted hemagglutinin for hemolysin. This means the published mouse data does not support the vaccine formulation that Forge is proposing. |
| Citation Test (cont.) | **FAIL (Surveyor Correction #1).** Forge states hemagglutinin is "necrophorum-specific." Kook et al. 2022 (46-strain comparative genomics) assigns hemagglutinin to funduliforme. The phenotypic data (Kanoe 1984/1989) contradicts the genomic data. Web search confirms these may be different proteins both annotated as "hemagglutinin" -- a filamentous hemagglutinin domain gene in funduliforme vs. the 19 kDa classical hemagglutinin. Until this is resolved by targeted PCR on liver abscess isolates, the third antigen in this vaccine is UNVALIDATED. |
| Strain Test | FomA: present in both subspecies (PASS). LktA: present in 45/46 strains (PASS with caveat -- 1 strain lacks it, and antigenic variation in PL4 region across bovine field isolates is UNKNOWN). Hemagglutinin: UNKNOWN -- subspecies distribution contradicted. |
| Embarrassment Test | **You are proposing a vaccine that failed commercially (Fusogard) with upgraded antigens, based on mouse data from a study that used different antigens than what you are proposing, with one of your three antigens having contradictory subspecies data. And the target disease requires 150+ days of protection against continuous challenge from a chronically damaged rumen wall.** The simplest way this fails: adjuvant technology cannot sustain high-titer multi-antigen antibodies for 5+ months, and the vaccine is overwhelmed by week 8 of grain feeding -- exactly like Fusogard. |
| Repetition Test | YES -- this repeats the Fusogard failure mode (vaccine overwhelmed on grain diet). The multi-antigen approach attempts to solve this by blocking adhesion upstream, but no evidence exists that anti-FomA antibodies reach effective concentrations at the rumen wall mucosal surface from systemic vaccination. |
| Clinical Endpoint Test | FAIL -- no cattle liver abscess endpoint data for any multi-antigen combination |

**What survives in Candidate 4:**
- FomA is a legitimately strong vaccine target: present in both subspecies, antibody blocking demonstrated in vitro (Kumar et al. 2015), strongest individual inhibitory effect in the 4-antibody cocktail (Amachawadi 2023)
- PL4 leukotoxin fragment generates neutralizing antibodies in cattle (Saginala 1997) -- the target is valid even if the vaccine format has failed
- The multi-antigen concept (adhesion blocking + toxin neutralization) is mechanistically sound

**What is fatally wrong:**
- The formulation cited (rFomA + rPL4 + rHemagglutinin) does not match ANY published study
- The hemagglutinin component has contradictory subspecies data
- ALL protection data is from mice that are insensitive to the primary virulence mechanism
- No evidence that systemic vaccination generates effective antibody concentrations at the rumen wall

**Verdict: WOUNDED (severe)**

Not killed outright because FomA and PL4 are individually validated targets. But the specific formulation Forge proposes is unsupported by the evidence cited. The vaccine must be reformulated: (1) replace hemagglutinin with a validated adhesin (OmpH, OmpA, or CSP from Amachawadi 2023), (2) acknowledge that the Amachawadi 2021 mouse study supports a 43K OMP + PL4 + H2 formulation, not the one Forge proposed, (3) accept that zero bovine grain-diet data exists for any multi-antigen combination. This remains the highest-potential vaccine candidate but it is farther from de-risked than Forge implies.

### Candidate 5: Anti-FomA Monoclonal Antibody

**Forge rank:** Tier 2

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citation Test | **FAIL (Surveyor Correction #3).** Forge claims anti-FomA mAb has a "dual mechanism: anti-adhesion + complement restoration via blocking Factor H binding." The Holm/Friberg 2008 study demonstrated that F. necrophorum binds Factor H, but the Factor H-binding protein was NOT identified as FomA. The "dual target" claim is speculation, not evidence. Strip this, and the candidate is a single-mechanism anti-adhesion mAb -- respectable but not differentiated. |
| Commercial Reality Test | **KILL-LEVEL CONCERN.** Current veterinary mAb cost: ~$350-500/dose (canine parvovirus). Required cost for feedlot cattle: <$10-15/dose. That is a 25-50x cost reduction requirement. With bovine IgG half-life of ~20 days (confirmed by PubMed PMID 16481343), you need 7-8 doses over 150 days. At even $15/dose x 8 doses = $120/head. Tylosin costs $3/head total for the entire feeding period. No feedlot operator will pay 40x the cost of tylosin for an unproven biologic. **Elanco's $130M Elwood facility expansion is focused on companion animal mAbs (canine parvovirus, canine dermatology). No food animal mAb product is in their public pipeline.** |
| Embarrassment Test | You are proposing a biologic that costs $120/head (at the aspirational price, 25x below current manufacturing cost), requires 8 injections over 5 months in a feedlot setting where cattle are processed through a chute at arrival and maybe once more for reimplant, and competes with a $3/head feed additive. The handling alone would cost more than the drug. |
| Species Test | In vitro (bovine cells) only. No in vivo bovine data. |

**Verdict: KILLED**

**What kills it: Economics.** The 25-50x cost reduction required from current veterinary mAb manufacturing has no precedent in the industry. Bovine IgG half-life of 20 days means 7-8 doses over 150 days. Even at aspirational pricing ($15/dose), the cost per head ($120) is 40x tylosin. The handling logistics of 8 injections in a commercial feedlot are operationally impossible. Elanco's mAb platform is companion-animal focused with no public food-animal pipeline. The dual-mechanism claim (Surveyor Correction #3) that was its key differentiator is unsubstantiated.

This is not a biology kill -- FomA is a good target. It is a commercial reality kill. If FomA-based protection is desired, put it in a vaccine (Candidate 4/12), not a mAb.

### Candidate 7: Mucoadhesive Rumen Wall Sealant

**Forge rank:** Not ranked (no tier)

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Matrix Test | **FAIL.** Surveyor flagged conflicting data on chitosan rumen stability. The rumen is pH-variable (5.0-7.0), physically hostile (continuous mixing, feed particle abrasion), and enormous (1 m2 surface area). Maintaining a polymer coating on damaged papillae under these conditions is material science fiction. |
| Stoichiometric Test | **FAIL.** To coat 1 m2 of rumen wall even at a 100-micron film thickness, you need ~100 mL of polymer. Achieving uniform coverage over the entire wall via a feed additive, with preferential coating of damaged areas, is physically implausible. Feed passage would strip any coating within hours. |
| Embarrassment Test | This is the biological equivalent of painting the deck of a ship while it is underwater. The rumen wall is continuously bathed in liquid, mechanically agitated, and renewed by epithelial turnover. A mucoadhesive coating applied orally would need to survive transit through the rumen liquid phase, find damaged areas preferentially, adhere to ulcerated tissue, and persist for hours -- all while the rumen contracts and feed particles scrape the surface. |
| Commercial Reality Test | Daily dosing of a polymer coating at sufficient volume to coat rumen wall would be expensive and logistically complex |

**Verdict: KILLED**

**What kills it: Physics.** The rumen environment destroys polymer coatings. The surface area is too large. The delivery challenge (oral to wall) is unsolvable. Forge's own risk assessment notes these problems but still proposes the candidate. The ex vivo adhesion test ($10K) is reasonable as a curiosity, but this will never be a commercial product.

### Candidate 12: Recombinant Leukotoxin PL4 Vaccine with Slow-Release Adjuvant

**Forge rank:** Tier 1

**Kill test results:**

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| 20-Year Test | **FAIL.** Leukotoxin vaccination has been known since the 1990s. Fusogard was commercialized and failed on grain diets. KSU vaccines never left challenge models. This is the third generation of leukotoxin vaccines. |
| Species Test | Cattle challenge model data exists (Saginala 1997, 80% protection). But the challenge model uses single bolus inoculation -- it does NOT replicate chronic translocation on grain diets. |
| Embarrassment Test | **The simplest way this fails: the adjuvant cannot sustain high-titer anti-leukotoxin antibodies for 150 days from 2-3 doses.** Forge's entire strategy hinges on adjuvant engineering, but no veterinary adjuvant has been demonstrated to maintain neutralizing antibody titers against a continuous challenge for 5+ months. The adjuvant comparison study ($50-70K) is the correct experiment, but if it fails, the entire vaccine strategy collapses. |
| Strain Test | Three lktA variants documented in human funduliforme isolates (Ohkuni et al. 2017). Antigenic variation in PL4 domain across bovine field isolates is UNKNOWN. If PL4 epitopes vary significantly between field strains, a single-variant vaccine will have coverage gaps. |
| Repetition Test | PARTIAL -- addresses Fusogard's failures (crude antigen, weak adjuvant) but faces the same fundamental challenge (dose overwhelm on grain diets) |
| Clinical Endpoint Test | FAIL -- no grain-diet feedlot trial of any purified recombinant leukotoxin vaccine |

**What survives:** Leukotoxin is a validated target. PL4 is the most immunoprotective fragment. Modern adjuvant technology (ISCO-MATRIX, Montanide ISA 201) is meaningfully better than what Fusogard used. Elanco has vaccine manufacturing infrastructure. **Verdict: SURVIVED**

Despite the 30-year history, this candidate has a plausible path forward because (a) the target is validated, (b) adjuvant technology has genuinely advanced since the 1990s, (c) the key experiment (adjuvant titer duration study, $50-70K) is well-defined and affordable, and (d) it is designed to be combined with burden-reducing candidates (MON+BX), not used alone. The combination strategy (Candidate 26) addresses the dose-overwhelm problem. This survives as the strongest vaccine candidate, not as a standalone.

## Portfolio-Level Assessment

### What survives and can form a coherent portfolio:

1. **MON+BX (#1)** -- burden reduction backbone (Stage 1, partial 3)
2. **rPL4 vaccine (#12)** -- immune evasion defeat (Stage 6)
3. **Triple stack (#26)** -- the combination hypothesis (#1 + #12 together)
4. **Hindgut prebiotic (#9)** -- the unaddressed 24% (Stage 5, contingent on KE#1)
5. **FP-100 MIC test (#27)** -- cheap shot at a potentially transformative narrow-spectrum antimicrobial (Stage 3)

This is a 5-candidate core portfolio. Everything else is wounded and needs specific data before it can be relied upon.

### The portfolio's fundamental vulnerability:

**Every surviving candidate that targets F. necrophorum faces the same 30-year-old problem: how do you sustain protection against continuous bacterial challenge from a chronically damaged rumen wall for 150+ days on a grain diet?**

MON+BX partially addresses this by reducing the challenge volume. The rPL4 vaccine addresses it if adjuvant technology can sustain titers. The combination (#26) is the bet that partial burden reduction + partial immune support = sufficient protection. But this bet has never been tested, and the cost is 8-12x tylosin.

The portfolio has no answer for what happens if the vaccine fails to sustain titers on grain diets -- which is exactly what happened to Fusogard.
