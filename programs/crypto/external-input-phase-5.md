## WHAT'S MISSING FROM THE PORTFOLIO

### Gap 1: Prophylaxis / Prevention

The entire portfolio is treatment-focused. Every asset is deployed *after* infection is established. But the economics of cryptosporidiosis are driven by the fact that **100% of calves on endemic farms are exposed** and **22-34% develop clinical disease.** A prophylactic approach — reducing the attack rate from 30% to 5-10% — could be more commercially valuable than any treatment.

**What I'd add:** A serious evaluation of **environmental decontamination** approaches. The memo mentions oocyst resistance to chlorine but doesn't explore:
- Hydrogen peroxide / peracetic acid protocols (some evidence of oocyst inactivation)
- UV treatment of water sources
- Ozone treatment of calf housing
- Ammonia-based bedding treatments

These are not drugs. They're management tools. They're squarely in Cargill's capability set. And they address the R0 problem — reducing transmission — rather than treating established infection.

### Gap 2: The Immune Maturation Window

The memo correctly identifies that the neonatal immune gap (immature CD4+ T cells, inadequate IFN-γ) is a core barrier. But no asset in the portfolio directly addresses immune maturation. The "KE#1 Phase B" experiment (drug + IFN-γ) is mentioned as a contingency but not developed.

**What I'd add:** A serious look at **immunomodulatory approaches** that accelerate or supplement neonatal immune maturation:
- **Recombinant bovine IFN-γ** administered alongside antiparasitic treatment (the KE#1 concept, but as a product, not just an experiment)

- **IL-22 agonists** or **NOD2 agonists** mentioned in the fallback logic but not developed as primary candidates

The memo's own analysis shows that drug efficacy depends on immune cooperation. If you can't accelerate immune maturation, you're always fighting with one hand tied behind your back.
