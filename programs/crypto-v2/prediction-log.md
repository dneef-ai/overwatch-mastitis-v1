## Prediction 1: Luminal Drug Delivery Is Superior to Systemic for C. parvum

**Prediction:** Oral non-absorbable compounds that reach C. parvum from the intestinal lumen through the parasitophorous vacuole membrane will achieve >2x greater parasite reduction compared to systemically absorbed compounds at equivalent molar doses in the niche.

**Reasoning:** The intracellular-but-extracytoplasmic niche positions C. parvum closer to the intestinal lumen than to the host cell cytoplasm. The parasitophorous vacuole membrane faces the extracellular environment, and paromomycin/geneticin have been shown to inhibit C. parvum WITHOUT trafficking through the host cell cytoplasm. This suggests luminal access to the niche is more direct than cytoplasmic access. If true, this restructures the entire portfolio toward non-absorbable oral compounds and away from cell-permeable small molecules.

**Testable by:** KE#1 — Ussing chamber drug access study comparing mucosal vs. serosal drug application on infected calf ileum.

**What changes if WRONG:** If systemic delivery is equivalent or superior, BKIs and PDE inhibitors (designed as absorbed small molecules) become the lead platform. Antibody-based approaches are deprioritized.

**Confidence:** 60%
**Status:** UNTESTED

## Prediction 2: Maternal Vaccination Against GP40 Will Reduce Diarrhea Duration But NOT Infection Rate or Peak Shedding

**Prediction:** Vaccinating dams with GP40-based subunit vaccines to boost colostral antibodies will reduce the DURATION and SEVERITY of diarrhea in calves by 30-50%, but will NOT significantly reduce the infection rate (proportion of calves infected) or peak oocyst shedding intensity.

**Reasoning:** GP40 antibodies can partially neutralize sporozoite invasion in vitro, and field trials show shorter diarrhea episodes. However, colostral IgG is rapidly degraded in the intestinal lumen and does not persist at effective concentrations through the entire 2-week vulnerability window. Furthermore, the autoinfection cycle via thin-walled oocysts regenerates infection from within the host, bypassing luminal antibodies. The antibodies may slow initial invasion enough to reduce clinical severity but cannot prevent the autoinfection-driven amplification that sustains shedding. The rBoIL-12 paradox (strong immune stimulation fails to protect) suggests that even enhanced immune responses are insufficient against established infection.

**Testable by:** Head-to-head field trial comparing vaccinated vs. unvaccinated dams, measuring both clinical scores AND quantitative oocyst shedding profiles in their calves.

**What changes if WRONG:** If maternal vaccination reduces peak shedding by >80%, then the environmental reduction strategy becomes viable through vaccination alone, potentially without the need for direct anti-parasitic drugs. This would dramatically simplify the portfolio.

**Confidence:** 75%
**Status:** PARTIALLY TESTABLE — EU Bovicrypt field trials show reduced diarrhea duration but quantitative shedding data is limited

## Prediction 3: CDPK1 Bumped Kinase Inhibitors Will Fail Commercial Viability for Neonatal Calves Despite Biological Efficacy

**Prediction:** BKIs targeting C. parvum CDPK1 will demonstrate anti-parasitic efficacy in neonatal calf challenge models (>50% reduction in oocyst shedding) but will NOT reach commercial product status for neonatal calves within 5 years due to the combination of: (a) manufacturing cost exceeding the $10-15/dose threshold for production animals, (b) the need for daily oral dosing during the 1-2 week vulnerability window, and (c) the requirement for prophylactic (not therapeutic) use, which demands universal treatment of all calves rather than targeted treatment of sick calves.

**Reasoning:** BKI-1553 has shown efficacy in calf models, and newer pyrrolopyrimidine scaffolds (BKI-1649, BKI-1812, BKI-1814) show improved potency. However, the development path mirrors halofuginone — daily prophylactic dosing for 7 days, which is labor-intensive and costly. The COGS for a novel chemical entity with a complex synthesis route will likely exceed feed-additive economics. The primary commercial driver would need to be either: (1) dramatically superior efficacy justifying premium pricing, or (2) reformulation as a single-dose sustained release product. Neither has been demonstrated.

**Testable by:** Phase-appropriate economic modeling using CRO synthesis quotes and field trial logistics costing.

**What changes if WRONG:** If BKI COGS can be brought below $5/treatment course, or if a sustained-release formulation enables single-dose administration, BKIs become the lead platform for Cargill. The portfolio shifts from multi-target combination to single-agent prophylaxis.

**Confidence:** 65%
**Status:** UNTESTED — requires COGS analysis

## Prediction 4: The Rate-Limiting Step for Clinical Severity Is Autoinfection, Not Primary Invasion

**Prediction:** The clinical severity of cryptosporidiosis in calves is primarily driven by the thin-walled oocyst autoinfection cycle (which amplifies infection internally after initial establishment) rather than by the quantity of primary oocyst ingestion from the environment. Specifically, an intervention that blocks autoinfection alone (without reducing environmental exposure or primary invasion) would reduce diarrhea severity by >60%.

**Reasoning:** Experimental challenge data shows that once the infectious dose threshold is crossed (~10 oocysts), clinical severity is independent of dose (Zambriski et al., 2013). This means the initial invasion event is just a spark — the autoinfection cycle is the gasoline. The ~20% of oocysts that are thin-walled excyst within the host and reinitiate the full cycle without any new environmental exposure. This internal amplification loop may be responsible for the exponential parasite expansion that overwhelms neonatal immunity and causes the most severe pathology. If autoinfection could be specifically blocked (e.g., by a luminal agent that prevents thin-walled oocyst excystation or sporozoite re-invasion within the gut), the infection might remain at a level the developing immune system can manage.

**Testable by:** Comparison of parasite burden dynamics in a model where primary infection is allowed but autoinfection is specifically inhibited (e.g., by continuous luminal anti-sporozoite antibody infusion starting 48h post-primary infection, after initial parasites are established but before autoinfection cycles amplify).

**What changes if WRONG:** If autoinfection contributes <30% to total parasite burden and primary invasion from environmental oocysts dominates, then environmental decontamination and invasion-blocking approaches become more important than anti-replication drugs.

**Confidence:** 55%
**Status:** UNTESTED — requires controlled experiment isolating autoinfection contribution

## Prediction 5: Indomethacin + Oral Rehydration Will Be More Effective Symptomatic Treatment Than Any Current Anti-Parasitic

**Prediction:** A combination of indomethacin (COX inhibitor to block prostaglandin-mediated secretory diarrhea) plus glutamine-enriched oral rehydration solution will reduce diarrhea severity by >50% and improve weight gain during infection more effectively than halofuginone prophylaxis, despite having ZERO direct anti-parasitic activity.

**Reasoning:** Blikslager et al. (2001) showed in Ussing chambers with C. parvum-infected bovine ileum that indomethacin + glutamine/alanyl-glutamine FULLY restored Na+ and Cl- absorption despite severe villous atrophy. Gookin et al. (2008) demonstrated that prostaglandin-dependent secretory chloride flux is a major contributor to diarrhea in infected piglets, and indomethacin fully inhibited this secretory component. Meanwhile, halofuginone is cryptostatic, not curative, and its clinical benefit is modest. By directly targeting the secretory mechanism of diarrhea rather than the parasite itself, this symptomatic approach may produce superior clinical outcomes because: (a) it works regardless of parasite burden, (b) it takes effect immediately rather than requiring prophylactic timing, and (c) it addresses the component of diarrhea (secretory) that is most amenable to pharmacological intervention.

**Testable by:** Randomized controlled trial in naturally infected neonatal calves comparing:
- Group A: Halofuginone prophylaxis (standard protocol)
- Group B: Indomethacin (0.5 mg/kg oral BID) + glutamine-ORS from onset of diarrhea
- Group C: Combination of A + B
- Endpoints: diarrhea duration, severity score, weight at 21 days, mortality

**What changes if WRONG:** If the secretory component is minor in calves (vs. piglets where it was demonstrated), and the malabsorptive/inflammatory components dominate, then symptomatic treatment approaches are deprioritized and direct anti-parasitic drugs remain the priority despite their limitations.

**Confidence:** 50%
**Status:** UNTESTED — bovine in vivo trial needed; ex vivo bovine data is supportive but piglet model may not fully translate

### Prediction 6: Advancing Adaptive Immune Activation by 3-4 Days Would Reduce Disease Severity by >80%

**Prediction:** If neonatal calf adaptive immune activation (IFN-gamma-producing CD4+ T cells in ileal mucosa) could be advanced from day 6-7 to day 3-4 post-infection, clinical diarrhea severity would be reduced by >80% and peak oocyst shedding by >2 log10, even without any direct anti-parasitic drug.

**Reasoning:** The Martian's quantitative analysis shows that parasite burden at day 3-4 (~10^6) is approximately 10,000-fold lower than at day 6-7 (~10^10). The immune system successfully clears 10^10 parasites by day 14-21. If it engaged at 10^6, clearance would be dramatically faster and tissue damage dramatically less. The 4-order-of-magnitude reduction in challenge should translate to proportional reduction in villous atrophy, malabsorption, and secretory diarrhea.

**Testable by:** Adoptive transfer experiment — transfer primed CD4+ T cells from previously infected adult cattle into neonatal calves at birth, then challenge with C. parvum. If transferred cells engraft in gut mucosa and provide early IFN-gamma, disease severity should be dramatically reduced vs. controls.

**What changes if WRONG:** If early immune activation does not reduce disease severity (as rBoIL-12 suggested), then the bottleneck is not the immune timing gap but something else — possibly PGE2-mediated local immunosuppression, a qualitative deficiency in neonatal effector mechanisms, or a fundamental inability of the immune system to clear established intracellular-extracytoplasmic parasites regardless of timing. The portfolio would shift entirely toward direct anti-parasitic drugs.

**Confidence:** 55%
**Status:** UNTESTED

### Prediction 7: PGE2-Mediated Local Immunosuppression Explains the rBoIL-12 Paradox

**Prediction:** The failure of rBoIL-12 to protect calves despite stimulating IFN-gamma production is primarily due to prostaglandin E2-mediated suppression of effector function at the parasite-epithelial interface. Co-administration of a COX-2 inhibitor with rBoIL-12 will restore protective efficacy of immune stimulation.

**Reasoning:** PGE2 is produced at high levels in C. parvum-infected ileum (established in piglet model). PGE2 suppresses Th1 responses, inhibits dendritic cell maturation, and promotes regulatory T cell differentiation. rBoIL-12 successfully induced systemic IFN-gamma and CD4+ T cell expansion, but these effectors may be functionally suppressed at the infection site by PGE2. Indomethacin fully inhibits the secretory component of diarrhea in infected tissue — if it simultaneously relieves local immunosuppression, the dual effect (reduced diarrhea + enhanced immune clearance) would be greater than either mechanism alone.

**Testable by:** Factorial calf challenge study: (1) Infected control, (2) rBoIL-12 only, (3) COX-2 inhibitor only, (4) rBoIL-12 + COX-2 inhibitor. Measure both clinical scores AND local immune effector function (IFN-gamma, iNOS) in ileal biopsies.

**What changes if WRONG:** If COX-2 inhibition does not rescue rBoIL-12 efficacy, then the immune failure is not PGE2-mediated. Alternative explanations (parasite effectors actively suppressing immune signaling, qualitative defect in neonatal effector T cells, or the niche physically shielding the parasite from immune effectors regardless of activation state) would need investigation. Anti-parasitic drugs would remain the primary strategy.

**Confidence:** 40%
**Status:** UNTESTED

### Prediction 8: Dose-Independence of Severity Holds Even Below ID50

**Prediction:** Calves infected with doses below the ID50 for diarrhea (i.e., <10 oocysts) that DO develop patent infection will show the same disease severity and shedding intensity as calves infected with 10^3-10^6 oocysts, confirming that amplification dynamics fully dominate over initial inoculum size.

**Reasoning:** Zambriski et al. (2013) showed dose-independence from 25 to 10^6 oocysts but did not analyze severity conditional on infection establishment at sub-ID50 doses. If the kinetic mismatch model is correct, even a single successful sporozoite invasion should produce the same disease trajectory because exponential amplification and autoinfection make starting conditions irrelevant within 2-3 cycles (24-36h). The prediction is that the relationship between initial sporozoite count and eventual disease severity is literally zero once establishment occurs.

**Testable by:** Re-analysis of Zambriski et al. data stratifying by initial dose among calves that developed patent shedding, or a new challenge study with 1, 5, 10, 50, 500 oocysts measuring complete shedding kinetics.

**What changes if WRONG:** If very-low-dose infections produce milder disease, then the initial establishment phase matters more than the amplification phase. This would re-prioritize invasion-blocking approaches (anti-GP40 antibodies) over merogony suppression, and suggest that reducing environmental oocyst load could modulate individual disease severity even without reaching zero exposure.

**Confidence:** 70%
**Status:** PARTIALLY TESTABLE — Zambriski et al. data exists but published analysis does not stratify this way

### Prediction 9: Continuous Luminal Drug Presence for 14 Days Is Required; Pulsed Dosing Will Fail Due to Autoinfection Rebound

**Prediction:** Any luminal anti-parasitic agent given in a pulsed regimen (once or twice daily oral dosing) will show less than 50% of the efficacy of the same agent delivered continuously (e.g., via slow-release bolus or feed incorporation), because autoinfection via thin-walled oocysts re-seeds the infection during inter-dose troughs.

**Reasoning:** Thin-walled oocysts (~20% of production) excyst within the gut lumen continuously. During the 8-12 hour trough between oral doses, liberated sporozoites from thin-walled oocysts can invade new enterocytes, establish new parasitophorous vacuoles, and begin a new merogony cycle that is not affected by the next drug dose (because the new parasites are now in the niche). Each inter-dose gap allows one or more merogony cycles to initiate from autoinfection, effectively ratcheting the infection forward. The exponential dynamics mean even brief gaps in drug coverage are catastrophic — a 12h gap at 8x amplification per cycle means the parasite regains one full cycle of amplification.

**Testable by:** The Claude Opus-proposed prophylactic window experiment: paromomycin q12h continuous vs. q24h vs. pulsed (7 days on/7 days off) vs. slow-release. Compare total oocyst output AUC as primary endpoint.

**What changes if WRONG:** If pulsed daily dosing achieves similar efficacy to continuous delivery, then autoinfection rebound during inter-dose gaps is not as rapid as predicted. This would make daily oral dosing commercially viable and remove the requirement for sustained-release formulations. The product development path becomes dramatically simpler and cheaper.

**Confidence:** 65%
**Status:** UNTESTED

### Prediction 10: Environmental Oocyst Load Reduction of 90% Will NOT Reduce Individual Calf Disease Severity in the Same Calving Season

**Prediction:** Even if environmental oocyst contamination is reduced by 90% (1 log10) through management interventions (steam cleaning, desiccation, pen rotation), the incidence and severity of clinical cryptosporidiosis in calves born into that environment will not be significantly reduced in the same season, because the remaining 10% of oocysts still vastly exceeds the ID50.

**Reasoning:** A contaminated pen with ~10^8 viable oocysts (conservative for a facility that housed multiple infected calves) reduced by 90% still has 10^7 oocysts. The ID50 is ~10. A calf exploring its environment will encounter sufficient oocysts for infection regardless. Furthermore, the dose-independence of severity means that even if the calf encounters only 50 oocysts instead of 50,000, the resulting disease will be identical. Environmental control must achieve >6 log10 reduction to bring exposure below the ID50 threshold — a level of decontamination that is practically unachievable in an agricultural setting.

**Testable by:** Controlled study comparing calves born into standard-managed pens vs. pens subjected to intensive decontamination protocol (steam cleaning + desiccation + extended vacancy). Measure infection rate and disease severity in both groups.

**What changes if WRONG:** If 90% oocyst reduction significantly reduces disease severity, then the dose-response relationship in field settings is different from the experimental challenge data (possibly because field exposure is gradual/cumulative rather than bolus). This would elevate environmental management as a high-value, low-cost intervention and reduce the urgency for new anti-parasitic drugs.

**Confidence:** 75%
**Status:** UNTESTED

### Prediction 13: No Single-Agent Therapeutic (Given After Clinical Signs) Will Achieve >50% Reduction in Peak Oocyst Shedding

**Prediction:** No single drug — whether BKI, NTZ, azithromycin, or any novel compound — given therapeutically (starting after onset of diarrhea, day 3-5 post-infection) will reduce peak oocyst shedding by more than 50% in experimentally infected neonatal calves.

**Reasoning:** The failure analysis reveals a universal pattern: every compound tested therapeutically (post-symptoms) shows dramatically less efficacy than when given prophylactically. The kinetic explanation is definitive: at symptom onset, parasite burden is 10^8-10^10 and the autoinfection cycle is fully established. A drug must achieve >3-4 log10 parasite reduction to meaningfully affect outcomes at this burden. No anti-Cryptosporidium compound has demonstrated this level of kill in vivo. The MMV665917 piperazine compound reportedly reduced oocyst shedding by ~94% when given 2 days after severe diarrhea onset — but this is a single study with intensive supportive care, and the 94% reduction still leaves substantial shedding. The prediction is that no single agent will consistently achieve >50% peak shedding reduction in a properly controlled therapeutic-timing trial.

**Exception noted:** The MMV665917 result (Castellanos-Gonzalez et al., 2018) may challenge this prediction if replicated. If confirmed, it would be the first truly therapeutic anti-Crypto compound and would restructure the entire portfolio strategy.

**Testable by:** Controlled therapeutic-timing calf challenge study with the best available compound (BKI or MMV665917), started at day 4-5 post-infection (after diarrhea onset). Compare peak oocyst shedding and total AUC vs. untreated controls.

**What changes if WRONG:** If a single therapeutic agent achieves >50% peak shedding reduction post-symptoms, the "timing gap" bottleneck is less absolute than predicted, and therapeutic (not just prophylactic) drug development becomes commercially viable. This would be a game-changing finding that opens a much larger market (treat sick calves, not just prophylax all calves).

**Confidence:** 70%
**Status:** PARTIALLY TESTED — MMV665917 data suggests this may be wrong, but single study only

### Prediction 14: Combination of Moderate Merogony Suppression + Moderate Immune Acceleration Will Be Synergistic (>Additive)

**Prediction:** The combination of a merogony-suppressing drug (e.g., BKI at sub-optimal monotherapy dose) + an innate immune accelerator (e.g., oral beta-glucan or CpG ODN at birth) will produce >2x the clinical benefit of either intervention alone, because they attack the timing gap from opposite sides.

**Reasoning:** The Tribunal determined the bottleneck is the 3-7 day gap between parasite amplification and immune engagement. Moderate merogony suppression (50% reduction in replication rate) shifts the parasite's kinetic curve rightward by 1-2 days. Moderate immune acceleration (advancing immune activation by 2-3 days) shifts the immune response curve leftward. Applied together, these two shifts can close the gap from both sides. The effect should be synergistic (greater than additive) because the parasite burden at the moment of immune engagement determines clearance kinetics: reducing burden by even 1 log10 at the time immune effectors arrive makes clearance dramatically faster due to the exponential nature of both processes.

Quantitatively: if a drug alone reduces peak burden from 10^10 to 10^9 (1 log), and immune acceleration alone advances engagement by 2 days (encountering 10^8 instead of 10^10), the combination would mean immune effectors encounter approximately 10^7 parasites — a 1,000-fold easier clearance challenge than untreated. The prediction is that this 3-log reduction in challenge would translate to >80% reduction in clinical disease severity.

**Testable by:** Factorial calf challenge study: (A) Untreated, (B) BKI at 50% effective dose only, (C) Beta-glucan at birth only, (D) BKI + beta-glucan combination. Primary endpoints: diarrhea severity, peak oocyst shedding, weight at day 21. Synergy assessed by comparing combination effect to sum of individual effects.

**What changes if WRONG:** If the combination is merely additive (not synergistic), the timing gap model's prediction about kinetic interaction is too optimistic. This would mean each intervention pathway must independently achieve large effect sizes, making single-agent development more important than combination strategy. If the combination fails entirely, then neither merogony suppression nor immune acceleration alone or together can bridge the gap, and entirely novel approaches (e.g., breaking autoinfection, host-directed therapy) are needed.

**Confidence:** 50%
**Status:** UNTESTED — requires both a BKI and an immune accelerator to be available for the same calf trial

### Prediction 15: The Next Drug to Fail in Calf Crypto Will Fail Because of Insufficient Duration, Not Insufficient Potency

**Prediction:** The next anti-Cryptosporidium compound to enter and fail a neonatal calf efficacy trial (whether it is a new BKI scaffold, PDE inhibitor, IMPDH inhibitor, or any other mechanism) will fail NOT because it lacks in vitro potency against C. parvum, but because its formulation does not maintain effective luminal/tissue concentrations for the full 14-day vulnerability window.

**Reasoning:** The pattern across all 13 analyzed treatments is clear: the field has the right targets (CDPK1, IMPDH, ProRS, even aminoglycoside ribosomal targets) and compounds with adequate in vitro activity. What every compound lacks is SUSTAINED DELIVERY. Halofuginone works when given daily from birth but requires 7 days of manual oral dosing. Paromomycin eliminates shedding at 100 mg/kg but requires 11 days of twice-daily dosing. BKIs show proof-of-concept but need daily administration. The pharmaceutical industry's habit of optimizing potency (lower IC50) while ignoring duration-of-action (sustained PK at the site of infection for 14 days) is the root cause of pipeline failure. The next compound will almost certainly have a sub-micromolar IC50 and excellent efficacy in 5-day mouse models, and then fail in the 21-day calf model because drug levels drop below the effective threshold before the vulnerability window closes.

**Testable by:** Monitor the next 3 compounds to enter calf efficacy trials. For each, document (a) in vitro potency, (b) treatment duration in trial protocol, (c) whether effective drug levels are maintained for 14 days, (d) efficacy outcome. The prediction is that >=2 of 3 will have adequate potency but insufficient duration.

**What changes if WRONG:** If the next compound fails due to genuine lack of potency (high IC50, poor target engagement, inactive against C. parvum), then the target identification problem is worse than the delivery problem, and Forge should prioritize novel target discovery over formulation engineering. This would also suggest that validated in vitro targets (CDPK1, IMPDH, PDE1) may not translate to the in vivo niche environment.

**Confidence:** 75%
**Status:** PROSPECTIVE — will be evaluated as pipeline compounds enter calf trials

### Prediction 17: Aldolase Is the Non-Redundant Glycolytic Chokepoint and Its Inhibition Will Be Rapidly Lethal

**Prediction:** C. parvum aldolase inhibition will kill intracellular parasites within 12-24 hours (one merogony cycle), faster than any other metabolic target, because aldolase is the sole non-redundant enzyme in glycolysis — the parasite's ONLY ATP source. Specifically, aldolase inhibition will be more rapidly lethal than glucose transporter inhibition (which is compensated by amylopectin stores) and more rapidly lethal than IMPDH inhibition (which takes multiple replication cycles to deplete nucleotide pools).

**Reasoning:** The 2024 Striepen lab genetic data established that CpGT1, CpGT2, and hexokinase are all individually dispensable, but aldolase is essential. First-principles analysis reveals why: aldolase sits downstream of ALL glucose entry points (external transport via CpGT1/GT2, hexokinase-mediated phosphorylation, AND glycogen phosphorylase-mediated amylopectin mobilization). No pathway bypasses aldolase. Furthermore, C. parvum has no mitochondrial respiration, no TCA cycle, and no alternative ATP source. Aldolase blockade = complete energy failure = rapid death. This should be faster-acting than nucleotide synthesis blockade (IMPDH) because ATP depletion is immediately lethal while nucleotide depletion requires dilution through cell division.

**Testable by:** (1) Obtain or synthesize a CpAldolase inhibitor (starting from T. brucei aldolase inhibitor scaffolds, adapted based on CpAldolase homology model). (2) Time-course comparison: CpAldolase inhibitor vs. CpIMPDH inhibitor vs. BKI-1553, each at 10x IC50, measuring parasite viability at 6h, 12h, 24h, 48h. Prediction: CpAldolase inhibitor shows >80% kill at 12h; IMPDH and BKI show <50% kill at 12h but catch up by 48h.

**What changes if WRONG:** If aldolase inhibition is NOT rapidly lethal (parasites survive >48h), then C. parvum has an undiscovered alternative energy source, or the parasite enters a metabolically quiescent state when glycolysis is blocked. This would be a fundamental revision of the C. parvum energy metabolism model and would reduce enthusiasm for glycolytic targets generally.

**Confidence:** 55%
**Status:** UNTESTED — requires CpAldolase inhibitor development and comparative time-course

### Prediction 18: Forced Myb-M Overexpression/Stabilization Will Create a Parasitic "Death Switch"

**Prediction:** A compound that stabilizes or activates Myb-M (the OPPOSITE of inhibition) will force premature sexual commitment of ALL parasites into terminally differentiated males, ablating asexual amplification within one merogony cycle. This "death switch" approach — forcing the parasite into irreversible terminal differentiation — will be MORE effective than Myb-M inhibition, because inhibition leaves asexual parasites intact (just blocks oocyst formation), while forced activation CONVERTS asexual parasites into non-replicating males that die.

**Reasoning:** The 2024 discovery demonstrated that conditional overexpression of Myb-M induced the male pathway in ALL parasites, leading to expression of male-specific genes and formation of terminally differentiated males, ablating further parasite replication and growth. This is a unique vulnerability: C. parvum's sexual commitment is a one-way gate controlled by a single transcription factor. Stabilizing that transcription factor triggers irreversible differentiation. No other anti-infective strategy exploits forced terminal differentiation of a pathogen. **Testable by:** (1) Identify the mechanism of Myb-M protein turnover (likely proteasomal degradation). Alternatively, if the parasite can revert from male commitment back to asexual growth under metabolic stress, the terminal differentiation is not truly irreversible, and the strategy becomes merely a growth delay rather than a cure.

**Confidence:** 35%

### Prediction 19: Existing Plasmodium PKG Inhibitors Will Show Cross-Activity Against CpPKG and Block Merozoite Egress
